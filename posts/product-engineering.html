Product engineering

Thesis: a lot of management in software engineering focuses on technical rather than business problems, leaving business discussions to “business”, or, more recently, to “product”. I’m curious 1/ why, 2/ if that’s a modern phenomenon (as much as it could be considering the age of the industry itself), and 3/ what should engineering managers actually do in different environments.

IT started as a business support function (to provide better business insights, automate some processes), but the core business innovations were happening outside the “computers”. Of course, over the last few decades we are undergoing a transformation, where software is becoming more and more important, and in many companies it has become the business itself.

However, with software eating the world (I actually don’t like this phrasing, it assumes that it’ll only be software, while I think hardware will always play a huge part in it, it’ll just be more integrated with software, so software integrates the world). Innovation is moving into the software layer, but without the underlying layers there will be nothing to build on top of.

An IT as a support function is fine to operate in a “ticketing” environment where things are regulated or already defined. Innovation is happening at the core of business processes, and business is not growing by optimising and investing into software. I think that’s where the “just wait for business to tell what to do” mindset comes from.

At the same time, the software becomes more sophisticated. Everyone needs to spend more and more time just to stay at the same level of understanding. This drives specialisation, and more vertical knowledge is required. Amazon was one of the first companies that I know of that separated roles of product and engineering because it was hard to find people that were good at both. It’s fine for IC to specialise, and as a manager you probably should too, but going outside of the area of just engineering is your responsibility.

I started my career in a few mechanical engineering companies that were producing “real” objects. Of course, the scale might have been different and the bigger the company the bigger and more specialised departments are. But there was no such thing as simply engineering. There was not some magical product or business just coming and telling what to do. Engineering was product and business. Of course, people specialise in different things and complexity always brings specialisation. But to be effective ,,, For some reason, the separation is higher when working with software, where engineering is surprised that it’s not possible to just work

This brings such points as engineering for the sake of engineering

I think scrum might have disproportionately contributed to this mindset by having the role of “requirements” and “priorities” strictly given to a magical product owner.

I also looked at the history of management in technology (back then tech had little to do with information, and software was yet to start embedding itself into all the parts of production systems) What’s fascinating that already back then author has established evolving factors of management roles and stated that it’s not only about science and engineering, but as much about understanding human capital, marketing and economics.

The main task of an engineering team is to deliver a product of course. And your goal as a leader is to ensure this delivery. But if the business you are working on has engineering at its core (engineering is a profit centre rather than cost centre), your responsibilities stretch much further than just engineering. You need to understand people, their motivations and build connections (this aspect is more universally accepted now), understand where is your valued add happening in business and marketing cycle (depending on the company you might have product and marketing managers leading those directions, but that’s your role to build those connections and work together with them on the engineering and business strategies).
